Busy Bus

Sumarry

Busy Bus is a real-time travel companion.
It gives users simple and necessary information according to real-time scenario. It helps users to schedule trip, navigate walk, find bus and get off at the right time.

Role
UX Designer

Tools
Figma, html/css, photoshop

Problem

Current transit apps are like stock dashboard, push too much information to the users at one time.

It’s difficult for users to understand complicated info and figure out what they need to do.

Solution

Busy Bus is a real-time travel companion that respond to every bus travel user scenario. It helps users to schedule trip, navigate walk, find bus and get off at the right time.

User Survey

I conducted user survey with 22 and get 18 valid responses. I found 2 sections of user behaviors: temporary travellers need more help to find nearby stops, get walking navigations and get step-by-step transit guidance, whereas routine riders don’t need to be over-reminded of stop locations, but are more sensitive to timetable management and route-change alert.

Considering current transit App usage, the survey revealed a meaningful gap between what features the users are mostly using and their potential need. Planing transit, finding fastest route and calculating travel time are the current provided features, whereas users desire real-time bus timetable, step by step walking navigation and alarm reminder of next action.

Competitive Analysis

I studied 2 major competitor Apps: Citymapper and Transit. There detailed SWOT analysis are as below.

Summarizing opportunities for newcomers, It’s all about balance. the current public transit app are arbitrary about a single feature that they want to highlight.

A newcomer should consider two different user categories -- curiosity-driven travelers and local routine riders -- balance their different needs and balance between data soundness and interface simplicity: users don’t want to get lost in a too overlapping spider-web; they want to see multiple information delivered with neat and clear format.

User Stories

According to competitive analysis and survey, I distinguished two categories of users with obvious behavior difference: temporary travellers and routine riders.

Then I tried to compare them and find overlap. I found some of their most important needs are well overlapped, I could tell the overlapped needs as they are most common stories for both types of users.

There are slightly difference in the rest of important needs for both groups separately, so I defined all these stories medium-high important. Below medium importance level, the stories become trivial and individual, so I defined all of them as low importance to avoid feature creep.

Testing & Adjustment

Based on the first-version wireframe, I tested 3 users with 3 tasks to complete, according to analysis of testing results, I adjusted original design.

Based on user behavior showed in the following 3 tasks:

Task 1 Please check at what time you should set off for a specific final destination.

  • Users understand the real-time set off notice
  • Users understand timelines of same number busses coming at different time
  • Users are unsure and confused with detailed icons, numbers, and meanings of texts.
  • Users misunderstand the time ruler

Task 2 Please open the real-time timetable of next coming bus near you.

  • Users follow the expected workflow
  • Users do not expect to open walking navigation from the dashboard
  • Users suggest the coming bus graph should be arranged in a direction that buses go from top to bottom.
  • Users expect timetable of a single bus and the info is missed

Task 3 Please get information to help you get off the vehicle when riding.

  • Users follow the expected workflow
  • Users understand the real-time get-off notice
  • User don’t understand the call-to-action ‘go’ button.
  • Drag-left for next step is not obvious.

Conclusion

Information involved in a public transit app can be as complicated as a stock dashboard.

To make a truly usable transit app, Busy-bus strategy is to provide necessary information once at one scenario and to keep the interface as simple as possible.
